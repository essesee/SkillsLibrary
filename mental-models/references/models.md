# 10 Mental Models Reference

## 1. The Map is Not the Territory
Our models of reality aren't reality itself. What data are we relying on, and where might it diverge from ground truth? Are we confusing the representation with the thing itself?

## 2. Circle of Competence
What do we actually know well here vs. what are we guessing at? Where are the edges of our expertise? Operating outside this circle compounds risk.

## 3. First Principles Thinking
Strip away assumptions. What are the fundamental truths? Build up from there instead of reasoning by analogy. Question inherited wisdom.

## 4. Thought Experiment
What if we pushed this to an extreme? What if the opposite were true? What does the edge case reveal? Reductio ad absurdum.

## 5. Second-Order Thinking
What happens after the first effect? What are the consequences of the consequences? Most people stop at the immediate result.

## 6. Probabilistic Thinking
What's the actual likelihood? Are we anchoring on a single scenario? What does the distribution of outcomes look like, not just the best case?

## 7. Inversion
Instead of asking "how do we succeed?", ask "how do we fail?" List failure modes and avoid them. Inverse thinking reveals hidden risks.

## 8. Occam's Razor
Among competing explanations, the simplest one is most likely correct. Are we overcomplicating this? Prefer fewer moving parts.

## 9. Hanlon's Razor
Don't attribute to malice what can be explained by incompetence, ignorance, or misaligned incentives. Avoid assuming bad intent.

## 10. Chesterton's Fence
Before removing something, understand why it exists. If you can't explain the reason for the current state, you don't yet understand the problem well enough to change it. Respect institutional knowledge.
