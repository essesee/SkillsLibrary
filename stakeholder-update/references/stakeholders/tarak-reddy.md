## Communication Voice Profile

- In Slack DMs, genuinely casual: "Nothing like meeting in the office walking to lunch and engaging in small talk"

### Sentence Style
- Extremely concise in replies. Often 1-2 sentences max: "We will get over to you by COB tomorrow. Thanks"
- "Per our conversation on the Sabre call, please share the report you mentioned. Thanks" — request + context in one line
- In announcements, uses structured formats with bold emphasis and tables
- Never rambles. Every sentence has a purpose.

### Vocabulary & Jargon
- Business/operations language: "licensee," "cutover," "go-live," "migrate," "capacity," "booking volumes"
- Thinks in dollars and time: "1 booking a day... 100 minutes... cut to 2 minutes from 5... an hour of saving which pays for $30"
- Uses "clubs" (not "clients" or "customers"), "agents" (not "users"), "federation"
- Comfortable with product names: DI, TripBuilder, Sabre, Travelport, GDS

### Openers & Closers
- Formal: "Hello Everyone -" / "Hi {name} -"
- Quick: "Hi {name} -" then straight to content
- Signs off: "Thanks" (quick) or "Thanks for your continued support and partnership. Tarak Reddy" (formal)
- Never uses "Best," "Regards," or "Cheers"

### How He Asks Questions
- Direct delegation: "please share the report you mentioned"
- Tags people by name for accountability: "@Tim Carter and team started the above — can you add other tickets?"
- Uses @channel when he needs group response: "Can you all directly slack me your feedback?"

### How He Prefers to Receive Information
- **BLUF first** — lead with the headline, then supporting detail if needed
- **Numbers over narrative** — if you can quantify it, do. He'll build the business case himself.
- **Escalation-ready framing** — "here's the blocker, here's the impact, here's what I need from you" format
- **Tables/structured data** for anything involving schedules, launches, or comparisons
- **Short is better** — he'll ask follow-up questions if he wants more detail

### "Match This" Examples
- "We will get over to you by COB tomorrow. Thanks"
- "Per our conversation on the Sabre call, please share the report you mentioned."

- Don't present problems without framing whether they need his action or are just FYI
- Don't use engineering jargon (sprint points, CI/CD, refactor)
- Don't be vague on timelines — he manages launch schedules and needs dates
- Don't bury asks — if you need something from him, put it first
